================================================================================
                    SECTION 1: PROGRAM OVERVIEW
================================================================================

WHAT IS THIS PROGRAM?
=====================
This is a Basic Life Support (BLS) training program that teaches healthcare
providers how to:
• Recognize cardiac arrest quickly
• Perform high-quality CPR (chest compressions + rescue breathing)
• Use an Automated External Defibrillator (AED) safely
• Save lives during cardiac and breathing emergencies

BLS is the first level of care given to someone whose heart has stopped or
who is not breathing.

WHY IS THIS IMPORTANT?
======================
Cardiac arrest is time-critical:
• Every minute without CPR, survival decreases by 7-10%
• With immediate CPR, survival can increase by 2-3 times
• 1 out of every 4 cardiac arrests happen at home
• In the United States, more than 350,000 out-of-hospital cardiac arrests
  occur yearly

KEY FACT: High-quality CPR started within 3 minutes can double or triple
survival chances.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
===================
After completing this program, you will be able to:
1. Recognize cardiac arrest and activate emergency response
2. Perform high-quality chest compressions (100-120/min, 2 inches deep)
3. Give effective rescue breaths (1 breath every 6 seconds)
4. Use AED correctly and safely
5. Perform CPR for adults, children, and infants
6. Handle special situations (drowning, opioid overdose, choking)
7. Work effectively in teams during resuscitation
8. Minimize interruptions in chest compressions

================================================================================
                    MODULE 1: INTRODUCTION TO CARDIAC ARREST & BLS
================================================================================

A. INTRODUCTION
===============
Cardiac arrest means the heart has stopped pumping blood. The person is:
• UNCONSCIOUS (not responding)
• NOT BREATHING (or only gasping)
• NO PULSE (heart not pumping)

BLS (Basic Life Support) is what you do IMMEDIATELY to save their life before
advanced help arrives.

Simple analogy: Think of the heart as a pump. When it stops, you must manually
push to keep blood flowing to the brain and lungs.

B. LEARNING OBJECTIVES
======================
By the end of this module, you will:
1. Understand what cardiac arrest is
2. Know the Chain of Survival (6 links)
3. Recognize the signs of cardiac arrest
4. Know when to start BLS

C. BASIC CONCEPTS
=================

1. WHAT IS CARDIAC ARREST?
--------------------------
NORMAL HEART:
• Heart pumps blood
• Person is conscious
• Person breathes normally
• Pulse is felt

CARDIAC ARREST:
• Heart stops pumping
• Person is unconscious
• Not breathing or only gasping
• No pulse

COMMON CAUSES:
• Heart problems (most common)
• Drowning
• Choking
• Drug overdose (especially opioids)
• Severe injury
• Electric shock

2. THE CHAIN OF SURVIVAL (6 LINKS)
----------------------------------
LINK 1 → LINK 2 → LINK 3 → LINK 4 → LINK 5 → LINK 6
  │         │         │         │         │         │
  │         │         │         │         │         └─ Recovery
  │         │         │         │         └─ Post-cardiac arrest care
  │         │         │         └─ Advanced care
  │         │         └─ Early AED
  │         └─ High-quality CPR
  └─ Recognize emergency & activate EMS

Each link is critical:
1. Recognize emergency & activate EMS — Call for help immediately
2. High-quality CPR — Start compressions right away
3. Early AED — Use defibrillator as soon as possible
4. Advanced care — EMS/hospital takes over
5. Post-cardiac arrest care — Hospital treatment
6. Recovery — Support after survival

3. SIGNS OF CARDIAC ARREST
--------------------------
Check these 3 things simultaneously (within 10 seconds):

SIGN 1: RESPONSIVENESS
• Tap shoulder + shout — no response?

SIGN 2: BREATHING
• No breathing OR only gasping?

SIGN 3: PULSE
• No pulse felt within 10 seconds?

AGONAL BREATHING (gasping) is NOT normal breathing — it's a sign of cardiac
arrest.

D. STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURES
==========================

BLS ADULT ALGORITHM FOR HEALTHCARE PROVIDERS

BOX 1: Verify Scene Safety
• Make sure the area is safe for you and the victim

BOX 2: Check Responsiveness
1. Tap the person's shoulder
2. Shout: "Are you okay?"
3. If NO response → proceed

BOX 2 (Continue): Shout for Help
1. Shout for nearby help
2. Activate emergency response system (call 911/EMS)
3. Get an AED and emergency equipment (or send someone)

BOX 3: Check Breathing & Pulse (simultaneously, within 10 seconds)
1. Look for NO breathing or only gasping
2. Check pulse (carotid artery for adults)
3. DECISION:
   ✓ Pulse felt + breathing normally → Monitor until EMS arrives
   ✓ Pulse felt + NO breathing → Rescue breathing (1 breath/6 seconds)
   ✓ NO pulse + NO breathing/gasping → START CPR

BOX 4: Start CPR
1. Perform cycles of 30 compressions + 2 breaths
2. Use AED as soon as available

BOX 5-8: AED Use (see Module 4 for details)

E. TABLES
=========

BLS QUICK REFERENCE TABLE

SITUATION                     ACTION
----------------------------------------------------------------------
No pulse, no breathing        Start CPR (30:2)
Pulse present, no breathing   Rescue breathing (1 breath/6 sec)
Pulse present, breathing      Monitor until EMS arrives
AED arrives                   Turn on, follow prompts
Shockable rhythm              Give 1 shock, resume CPR 2 min
Non-shockable rhythm          Resume CPR 2 min

G. REAL-LIFE CLINICAL EXAMPLE
=============================

SCENARIO: You're working in a hospital lobby. A 65-year-old man suddenly
collapses.

WHAT YOU DO:
1. ✓ Verify scene is safe (no electricity, traffic, etc.)
2. ✓ Tap shoulder: "Mr. Johnson, are you okay?" — No response
3. ✓ Shout: "Help! Call 911! Get an AED!"
4. ✓ Check breathing: Only gasping (agonal breathing)
5. ✓ Check pulse (carotid): No pulse in 10 seconds
6. ✓ START CPR immediately — 30 compressions, 2 breaths
7. ✓ AED arrives → turn on, follow prompts
8. ✓ Give shock if advised → resume CPR immediately
9. ✓ Continue until EMS takes over

RESULT: Because you started CPR within 2 minutes, the patient survives with
good brain function.

H. COMMON MISTAKES
==================

MISTAKE 1: Not checking scene safety
WHY WRONG: You could get hurt
CORRECT: Check first, then act

MISTAKE 2: Taking too long to check pulse
WHY WRONG: Delays CPR
CORRECT: Check pulse in ≤10 seconds

MISTAKE 3: Not shouting for help
WHY WRONG: No backup comes
CORRECT: Shout immediately

MISTAKE 4: Treating gasping as normal breathing
WHY WRONG: Misses cardiac arrest
CORRECT: Gasping = NO breathing

MISTAKE 5: Stopping compressions too often
WHY WRONG: Blood flow stops
CORRECT: Minimize interruptions

MISTAKE 6: Not using AED ASAP
WHY WRONG: Defibrillation saves lives
CORRECT: Use AED when available

I. SAFETY TIPS
==============

1. Verify scene safety first — Never rush into dangerous areas
2. Use personal protective equipment (PPE) if available (mask, gloves)
3. Don't touch the patient when AED is analyzing or shocking
4. Stand clear during AED shock — say "CLEAR!"
5. Know your limits — Stop only when:
   • You see clear signs of life
   • AED says "analyzing"
   • EMS takes over
   • You're physically unable to continue

J. IMPORTANT POINTS TO REMEMBER
===============================

⚠ Cardiac arrest = no pulse, no breathing — start CPR immediately
⚠ Gasping is NOT normal breathing — it's a sign of cardiac arrest
⚠ Check pulse in ≤10 seconds — don't delay CPR
⚠ Call for help immediately — don't work alone
⚠ AED saves lives — use it as soon as available
⚠ Minimize interruptions in chest compressions

K. MODULE 1 SUMMARY
===================

• Cardiac arrest means the heart stopped pumping
• The 6-link Chain of Survival guides rescue care
• Check responsiveness, breathing, and pulse within 10 seconds
• Start CPR if no pulse + no breathing
• Use AED immediately when available
• Minimize interruptions in chest compressions

================================================================================
                    MODULE 2: HIGH-QUALITY CHEST COMPRESSIONS
================================================================================

A. INTRODUCTION
===============
Chest compressions are the MOST important part of CPR. They manually pump
blood to the brain and heart when the heart stops.

Simple explanation: You're using your hands to push the heart and squeeze
blood out to vital organs.

B. LEARNING OBJECTIVES
======================
By the end of this module, you will:
1. Know the correct hand position
2. Perform compressions at the right depth (2 inches) and rate (100-120/min)
3. Allow full chest recoil
4. Minimize interruptions
5. Use feedback devices when available

C. BASIC CONCEPTS
=================

1. WHAT MAKES COMPRESSIONS "HIGH-QUALITY"?

QUALITY         STANDARD
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Rate            100-120 compressions per minute
Depth           At least 2 inches (5 cm) for adults
Recoil          Allow full chest recoil (don't lean)
Interruptions   Minimize pauses (<10 seconds)
Force           Push hard, use upper body strength

SONG ANALOGY: Push at the rate of "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees (100-120 bpm)

2. WHY DEPTH MATTERS

DEPTH                   EFFECT
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Too shallow (<2 inches)  Blood doesn't reach brain
Correct (2 inches)       Good blood flow to vital organs
Too deep (>2.4 inches)   Risk of breaking bones

3. WHY RATE MATTERS

RATE                  EFFECT
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Too slow (<100/min)   Blood flow too slow
Correct (100-120/min) Optimal blood flow
Too fast (>120/min)   Chest doesn't recoil fully

4. FULL CHEST RECOIL

WHAT IS RECOIL? After each push, the chest returns to normal position.

WHY IT MATTERS: If you lean on the chest, blood doesn't flow back to the
heart → less blood pushed out next compression

CORRECT POSITION:
✓ Hands on chest, arms straight
✓ Body weight over hands
✓ Lift completely after each push

D. STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURES
==========================

HOW TO PERFORM CHEST COMPRESSIONS (ADULT)

STEP 1: Position Yourself
1. Place victim on firm, flat surface
2. Kneel beside victim's chest
3. Place knee near victim's shoulder

STEP 2: Hand Position
1. Place heel of one hand on center of chest (lower half of breastbone)
2. Place other hand on top of first hand
3. Interlock fingers (don't press on ribs)

STEP 3: Body Position
1. Stack shoulders directly over hands
2. Keep arms straight (elbows not bent)
3. Use upper body weight (not just arm strength)

STEP 4: Perform Compressions
1. Push hard (at least 2 inches deep)
2. Push fast (100-120 per minute)
3. Allow full recoil (chest returns completely)
4. Count: "1, 2, 3... 30"

STEP 5: Give 2 Breaths
1. After 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths
2. Then immediately resume compressions

STEP 6: Continue Cycle
1. Repeat 30:2 cycle
2. Minimize interruptions (<10 seconds)
3. Switch compressor every 2 minutes (if 2+ rescuers)

WHEN TO SWITCH COMPRESSORS

SITUATION                     ACTION
----------------------------------------------------------------------
2+ rescuers present           Switch every 2 minutes (5 cycles)
Compressor is tired           Switch immediately
AED analyzing                 Pause, then resume
Shock given                   Resume CPR immediately (2 min)

E. TABLES
=========

CPR COMPRESSION GUIDELINES BY AGE

AGE GROUP         HAND POSITION            DEPTH          RATE
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Adult             2 hands, center chest    2 inches       100-120/min
Child (1-8 yrs)   1 or 2 hands             2 inches       100-120/min
Infant (<1 yr)    2 fingers or 2 thumbs    1.5 inches     100-120/min

COMPRESSION-TO-VENTILATION RATIO

RESCUERS          ADULT          CHILD/INFANT
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Single rescuer    30:2           30:2
2+ rescuers       30:2           15:2 (healthcare providers)

G. REAL-LIFE CLINICAL EXAMPLE
=============================

SCENARIO: You're in a grocery store. A woman collapses. You're the only
responder. An AED is available 50 feet away.

WHAT YOU DO:
1. ✓ Check responsiveness: No response
2. ✓ Check breathing/pulse: No pulse, no breathing
3. ✓ START COMPRESSIONS IMMEDIATELY
4. ✓ Push hard: 2 inches deep
5. ✓ Push fast: 100-120/min (count "1, 2, 3...")
6. ✓ Full recoil: Lift hands completely after each push
7. ✓ After 30 compressions: Give 2 breaths
8. ✓ Return to compressions immediately
9. ✓ Continue until AED arrives

KEY POINT: You started compressions BEFORE getting the AED — this is correct!
Early CPR is critical.

H. COMMON MISTAKES
==================

MISTAKE 1: Pushing too shallow (<2 inches)
WHY WRONG: Blood doesn't reach brain
CORRECT: Push at least 2 inches

MISTAKE 2: Pushing too slow (<100/min)
WHY WRONG: Blood flow too slow
CORRECT: Push 100-120/min

MISTAKE 3: Leaning on chest (no recoil)
WHY WRONG: Heart doesn't fill
CORRECT: Lift completely after each push

MISTAKE 4: Stopping too long
WHY WRONG: Blood flow stops
CORRECT: Pauses <10 seconds

MISTAKE 5: Bent elbows
WHY WRONG: Less force
CORRECT: Keep arms straight

MISTAKE 6: Not using body weight
WHY WRONG: Compressions too weak
CORRECT: Use upper body weight

I. SAFETY TIPS
==============

1. Use firm surface — Don't do CPR on soft mattress
2. Watch for rib fractures — Rib breaks are common but CPR still saves lives
3. Use feedback devices — If available, use devices that tell you if
   compressions are correct
4. Switch every 2 minutes — Compressor gets tired; quality decreases
5. Don't stop for pulse check — Continue CPR; only stop for AED analysis/shock

J. IMPORTANT POINTS TO REMEMBER
===============================

⚠ Push HARD: At least 2 inches deep
⚠ Push FAST: 100-120 compressions per minute
⚠ Full RECOIL: Chest must return completely
⚠ MINIMIZE interruptions: Pauses <10 seconds
⚠ Use body weight: Not just arm strength
⚠ Switch every 2 minutes: Maintain quality

K. MODULE 2 SUMMARY
===================

• High-quality compressions = rate 100-120/min, depth 2 inches, full recoil
• Push hard and fast using upper body weight
• Allow full chest recoil after each compression
• Minimize interruptions (pauses <10 seconds)
• Switch compressors every 2 minutes if 2+ rescuers
• Rib fractures are common but don't stop CPR
